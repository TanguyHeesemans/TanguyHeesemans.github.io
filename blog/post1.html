Some thoughts on plant sciences

Apparently I am becoming a plant scientist, or at least a scientist that studies plants. Hence I thought it would worthwhile to reflect how I ended up here as initially I was trained as a chemist. First it would be good to point one my own beliefs regarding the natural world: anything can become interesting if one looks at it long enough (on a side note: how else do you convince yourself to look at evaporating water droplets for four years). Second I would like to point out that even though one changes subject, once underlying methods might not change too much. In my case, I am very inspired by the soft matter approach of things. As the grandfather of soft matter Pierre-Gilles de Gennes remarked in his inauguration speech for the Nobel Prize in Physics, soft matter is characterized by "the possibilty very simple experiments" (again, evaporating water droplets).

From this soft matter approach I take that one should always try to design experiments that as simple as possible. This comes in handy when you are pretty clumsy like me. These days a lot is possible. Especially, in the biological sciences it seems that ever increasing complexity of experimental design coupled with the rise of all the omics fields makes that even I (let's keep this one personal to not offend anyone) don't quite understand what it is that I am studying. The amount of standardized protocols certainly does not help either. Hence sometimes I like to take a step back and ask myself: "is all this really necessary?" Usually, after looking at the suspended ceiling for more time than I would like to admit to my supervisors, I come up with an easy experiment that still measures what I would like to see.

Let's not fool ourselves too much. Science takes time. So does designing a good experiment (simple or complex). In the words of Richard Powers: "Information may travel at the speed of light, but meaning spreads at the speed of darkness." As a scientist I aim to spread meaning, not just throw more information in the air.
